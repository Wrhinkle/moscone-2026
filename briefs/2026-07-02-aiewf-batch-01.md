## Angle: Construction / business operations

### 1. Stop Building Chatbots for the Back Office — Build Evaluators
- **Core thesis:** The useful shape of back-office AI is not conversational. It's an evaluator: messy operational context in, structured state + missing-item detection + human-approvable next actions out. Chat is an interface mismatch for work that has a checklist.
- **Sources:** Reducto, Extend, Bem, Wisedocs (document extraction); HumanLayer (approval gates); the Job Packet Readiness Auditor demo; papers: REAgent, From Plan to Action, Governance by Construction for Generalist Agents.
- **Why now:** Document-extraction vendors have made the parsing layer a commodity — the differentiation moved up a level, to judgment about readiness and risk. Most SMB "AI adoption" is still a chatbot bolted onto a CRM.
- **Practical angle:** A pattern any operator can copy: hardcode the checklist, let the model do extraction + gap detection, always end at a human approval point. Ship it as paste-text-in → structured-report-out, no integrations.
- **Outline:** (1) The failure mode: jobs move to production with paperwork holes. (2) Why a chatbot doesn't fix this. (3) The evaluator pattern: state → gaps → risk → next actions → JSON. (4) Walkthrough of the roofing job packet auditor. (5) Mapping to the research: this is plan compliance for operations. (6) Where the JSON goes next (tool calls into CRM/email).
- **Non-obvious because:** it inverts the industry default — the post argues the *interface* (evaluation report vs. conversation) is the design decision that matters, and grounds it in agent-planning research rather than product opinion.
- **Demo/project tie-in:** `job-packet-readiness-auditor` — already built; needs a public repo + one obfuscated job packet.
- **Research references:** REAgent (requirement extraction from messy input); From Plan to Action (agents verifying state against a plan); Governance by Construction (constraints built into the loop, not policy docs).
- **Compare/cite:** Reducto vs. Extend vs. Bem vs. Wisedocs on the axis "parsing API vs. workflow product vs. vertical solution" — and why the checklist layer above all of them is where an operator's edge lives.

### 2. The AI Intake Clerk: One Pattern for Every Paper-Heavy Business
- **Core thesis:** Subcontractor bills, insurance packets, permits, and change orders are the same problem wearing different clothes: unstructured intake → structured review packet → human approval. Build the pattern once and every document stream in the business becomes a variant.
- **Sources:** Reducto, Extend, Bem, Wisedocs, Auditoria AI (finance back office), Ramp (expense/procurement), Docusign + Ironclad (agreement workflows); concepts: back-office AI intake clerk, missing-information detection, operational risk scoring.
- **Why now:** The vendors above are converging on the same shape from different verticals — naming the shared pattern lets small operators build it locally instead of buying four point solutions.
- **Practical angle:** A reusable template: extraction schema + required-fields checklist + duplicate/risk heuristics + copy-ready follow-up message + accounting note. Reader swaps the schema per document type.
- **Outline:** (1) The pile of sub bills problem. (2) The intake-clerk pattern, field by field. (3) Instantiating it three times: sub bill, job packet, change order. (4) What stays human: approval, payment, customer contact. (5) When to graduate from paste-in demo to Merge/Airbyte-fed pipeline.
- **Non-obvious because:** it's a pattern-extraction post, not a tool post — the claim that four funded verticals (Reducto/Auditoria/Wisedocs/Ironclad) are the same loop with different schemas is the kind of map vendors don't draw.
- **Demo/project tie-in:** Sub Bill Intake Checker (backlog, ~2-hour build) as the second instantiation next to the existing packet auditor — the post's punchline is how little code changed between them.
- **Research references:** Do Agents Need Semantic Metadata? (what structure extraction actually needs); explainable agent outputs / human review loops framing.
- **Compare/cite:** Auditoria AI and Ramp (finance-native automation) vs. building the thin local version; Docusign/Ironclad as where approval workflows already live in businesses.

## Angle: Back-office AI / approval gates

### 3. Agents Should Prepare Decisions, Not Make Them
- **Core thesis:** The approval gate is an engineering primitive, not a compliance afterthought — and the stack for it already exists: durable workflow engines hold the agent's work in a pending state, a human-contact layer routes the decision, and the agent's job is to make the decision *easy*, not to make it.
- **Sources:** HumanLayer (human approval/contact layer for agents), Temporal + Inngest + Orkes (durable workflows), WorkOS + Scalekit (audit logs, enterprise identity); papers: Governance by Construction, CaMeLs Can Use Computers Too, ClawLess.
- **Why now:** Agent capability outran agent trust; the emerging security literature (CaMeLs, ClawLess) is converging with what workflow vendors shipped for other reasons. Nobody has written the practitioner synthesis.
- **Practical angle:** Concretely wiring an approval gate: agent output → durable workflow step with timeout/escalation → human channel (Slack/email) → signed decision → resumed execution, all auditable. A change order is the perfect worked example: the agent drafts the explanation and evidence list; a human approves the customer-facing action.
- **Outline:** (1) Why "human in the loop" is usually vaporware (a checkbox, not a system). (2) The three components: durable state, contact routing, audit trail. (3) Worked example: change-order explainer with a real gate. (4) What the security papers add: least privilege and constructed constraints. (5) Failure modes: approval fatigue, rubber-stamping, timeout defaults.
- **Non-obvious because:** it reframes governance from policy language to infrastructure choices, and points out that Temporal-style durability — built for microservices — is accidentally the right substrate for human latency (hours, not milliseconds).
- **Demo/project tie-in:** Change Order Explainer (from the Ridgeline idea set) with a real HumanLayer + Inngest gate — small enough to build, real enough to screenshot.
- **Research references:** Governance by Construction (constraints in the loop's structure); CaMeLs (system-level security for computer-use agents); ClawLess (agent security model).
- **Compare/cite:** Temporal vs. Inngest vs. Orkes on the axis "where does pending-human-decision state live"; HumanLayer as the routing layer; WorkOS audit logs as the paper trail.

## Angle: Agentic software engineering

### 4. Your Coding Agent Has Never Seen a Codebase Like Yours
- **Core thesis:** Coding-agent benchmarks are greenfield and task-shaped; real software work is brownfield and history-shaped. The gap between benchmark scores and on-the-job usefulness is mostly *codebase comprehension*, and a whole vendor tier exists to patch exactly that.
- **Sources:** Cognition, Factory, Qodo, OpenHands, poolside (coding agents); Sourcegraph, Greptile, Unblocked (codebase understanding); papers: Position: Coding Benchmarks Are Misaligned with Agentic Software Engineering, ProdCodeBench, SWE-EVO, FeatureBench, One Developer Is All You Need.
- **Why now:** 2025–26 saw a wave of papers (ProdCodeBench, SWE-EVO) explicitly attacking benchmark-reality mismatch, while the expo floor splits into "agents that write code" and "tools that explain your code to agents" — the split *is* the story.
- **Practical angle:** Before adopting a coding agent, run it on your own repo against production-derived tasks (a real closed ticket, a real refactor), not the vendor's demo. The post gives a 5-task self-benchmark recipe.
- **Outline:** (1) The benchmark scores vs. the Tuesday-morning ticket. (2) What the misalignment papers actually measured. (3) Why context tooling (Sourcegraph/Greptile/Unblocked) exists at all. (4) The self-benchmark recipe. (5) The one-person-squad case study and what it implies for small teams.
- **Non-obvious because:** it treats the existence of the codebase-comprehension vendor tier as *evidence* for the research position — reading the expo floor as a market confession.
- **Demo/project tie-in:** Run one open-source coding agent (OpenHands or Cline) against a small brownfield repo the reader can clone; publish the task list and transcripts.
- **Research references:** Position paper (the argument); ProdCodeBench (production-derived tasks); SWE-EVO (long-horizon evolution); One Developer Is All You Need (brownfield enterprise case study).
- **Compare/cite:** Cognition/Factory/Qodo on autonomy level; Sourcegraph vs. Greptile vs. Unblocked on what "context" means (search vs. review vs. tribal knowledge).

### 5. The Lights-Off Software Factory Is Mostly Plumbing
- **Core thesis:** The binding constraint on autonomous software delivery isn't model intelligence — it's the unglamorous integration layer: sandboxes, merge queues, flaky-test handling, review gates, and durable orchestration. The factory is an infrastructure project wearing an AI costume.
- **Sources:** E2B + Daytona + Coder + Modal (sandboxes/dev environments), Aviator (merge queues, flaky tests), Buildkite + CircleCI (CI), Sonar + Snyk + Meticulous (quality gates), Temporal (orchestration), Factory + Cognition (the agents); papers: Effective Strategies for Asynchronous Multi-Agent Collaboration in Software Engineering, Reproducible/Explainable/Effective Evaluations, SlopCodeBench.
- **Why now:** "Software factory" went from metaphor to product category (Factory the company, StrongDM-style internal factories) — but every public account of one working is dominated by harness engineering, not prompts.
- **Practical angle:** A bill of materials for a minimum viable factory loop: ticket intake → sandboxed implementation → tests → agent review → human gate → merge queue — with a named vendor or OSS option per slot, and which slots you can skip at what team size.
- **Outline:** (1) The dream and the demo-day version. (2) The seven slots in the loop. (3) Where loops actually break (flaky tests, merge conflicts, long-horizon drift). (4) The async multi-agent findings. (5) A minimum viable factory for a 2-person team.
- **Non-obvious because:** it argues the moat is boring — and predicts the winners look like CI companies, not model companies.
- **Demo/project tie-in:** Wire a single loop end-to-end on a toy repo: GitHub issue → OpenHands in an E2B sandbox → PR → automated review comment → human approval. Publish the config.
- **Research references:** Asynchronous Multi-Agent Collaboration (coordination strategies); SlopCodeBench (why unattended loops degrade); Reproducible Evaluations (how to know your factory works).
- **Compare/cite:** E2B vs. Daytona vs. Modal (sandbox tradeoffs); Aviator's merge-queue/flaky-test positioning as the load-bearing unglamorous piece.

### 6. Agents Get Worse the Longer They Work — Plan for It
- **Core thesis:** Long-horizon degradation ("slop accumulation") is a measured phenomenon, not a vibe — and the practical mitigations are project-management shaped: vertical slices, plan-compliance checks, fresh-context checkpoints, and tracing that catches drift early.
- **Sources:** papers: SlopCodeBench, SWE-EVO, From Plan to Action, How AI Coding Agents Shape Software Architecture; tools: Arize + Braintrust (agent tracing/evals), Sentry + Datadog (production monitoring), LangChain/LangGraph + Temporal (checkpointed execution).
- **Why now:** Teams are moving from single-task agents to multi-day agent projects, and hitting degradation walls the benchmarks now quantify.
- **Practical angle:** Concrete working agreements for long agent runs: slice size limits, a plan artifact the agent must re-verify against, when to reset context vs. continue, and what to trace to see drift before the diff review does.
- **Outline:** (1) What SlopCodeBench actually measured. (2) Why degradation happens (context pollution, architecture drift). (3) The architecture-shaping finding: agents don't just write worse code, they bend your system's shape. (4) Mitigations mapped to tools. (5) A degradation checklist for agent-heavy teams.
- **Non-obvious because:** the "agents shape architecture" angle — long-horizon agent work changes system design pressure, which almost nobody discusses outside the paper.
- **Demo/project tie-in:** Reproduce a mini SlopCodeBench: give one agent a 10-step iterative task, trace it with Braintrust, chart quality per step.
- **Research references:** SlopCodeBench (degradation curves); SWE-EVO (evolution tasks); From Plan to Action (plan-compliance gap); How AI Coding Agents Shape Software Architecture (drift direction).
- **Compare/cite:** Braintrust vs. Arize for step-level agent tracing; LangGraph checkpoints vs. Temporal durability as context-reset substrates.

## Angle: Evals & observability

### 7. The Demo Worked. Now Prove It Twice a Week.
- **Core thesis:** For small agent products, the eval stack is the product roadmap: a 20-case golden set plus tracing tells you more than any framework migration. Most operators need a spreadsheet's worth of evals, not an MLOps platform — but they need it *running*, not planned.
- **Sources:** Braintrust, Arize, Weights & Biases, Comet, Fiddler (eval/observability tier); Hamming AI (voice-agent evals); Neurometric, Andon Labs (eval/stress-testing labs); papers: Reproducible/Explainable/Effective Evaluations of Agentic AI, Toward Architecture-Aware Evaluation Metrics, RAG evaluation concepts.
- **Why now:** The eval vendor tier at AIEWF is as big as the model tier — a signal that reliability, not capability, is the current bottleneck. Meanwhile most small builders still ship on vibes.
- **Practical angle:** A minimal eval harness for a real small tool (the job packet auditor): 20 obfuscated packets with known verdicts, a scoring script, a regression run per prompt change. Total build: an afternoon.
- **Outline:** (1) Vibes-shipping and its failure story. (2) The golden-set method, sized for one person. (3) Building the 20-case set for the auditor (obfuscation recipe included). (4) When to graduate to Braintrust/Arize and what each adds. (5) What the reproducible-evals paper says big labs get wrong that you can get right at small scale.
- **Non-obvious because:** it argues *downscale*: the interesting move is translating enterprise eval practice to a one-person operation, opposite to every vendor's pitch deck.
- **Demo/project tie-in:** Eval harness for `job-packet-readiness-auditor` — doubles as the missing quality proof for post #1.
- **Research references:** Reproducible/Explainable/Effective Evaluations (what makes an eval trustworthy); Architecture-Aware Evaluation Metrics (why task pass/fail isn't enough).
- **Compare/cite:** Braintrust vs. Arize vs. W&B on the axis "experiment tracking vs. production tracing vs. eval-first"; Hamming AI as evidence evals are verticalizing (voice-specific).

The hard part isn't any component; it's deciding what deserves memory.
- **Sources:** Firecrawl (LLM-ready crawling), Exa (neural search), Bright Data + Apify + Oxylabs (data collection), Zep AI + Cognee (agent memory), Neo4j + FalkorDB + LanceDB (graph/vector storage); concepts: research-monitoring agents, weekly intelligence briefs, entity tracking; papers: Memory for Autonomous LLM Agents, GAM, Hierarchical Long-Term Semantic Memory for LinkedIn's AI Agents, Agentic Search in the Wild, "As We May Think."
- **Why now:** The memory-infrastructure tier just showed up at the expo (Zep, Cognee, FalkorDB all sponsoring) — memory stopped being a research topic and became a product category, which means the Memex is finally a build, not a paper.

## Angle: Tool-call-first productivity

### 9. Every Menu Is an API: Designing Software Where Agents Are Users
- **Core thesis:** The reliable path to computer-using agents isn't teaching models to click pixels — it's redesigning software so every action is a typed tool call. Games make this vivid: expose every system (inventory, crafting, dialogue) as callable functions and an agent can *play* without hallucinating mechanics. The same move makes business software agent-operable and auditable.
- **Sources:** Composio (tool integrations), Browserbase + Cua (browser/computer-use), E2B (sandboxes), Mastra + Pydantic (typed agent frameworks/structured outputs), The Browser Company + Yutori (agent-native browsing); papers: The Evolution of Tool Use in LLM Agents, Verifiable Software Worlds for Computer-Use Agents, CaMeLs, WebXSkill.
- **Why now:** Two camps are racing: computer-use (agents adapt to human UIs) vs. tool-call-first (software adapts to agents). The Verifiable Software Worlds paper gives the second camp its theory; the expo floor gives it its vendors.
- **Practical angle:** A design rule for anyone building software in 2026: ship the tool registry alongside the UI. Every user action = one structured, replayable, permission-scoped call. Auditability and undo fall out for free — for humans too.
- **Outline:** (1) The pixel-clicking dead end (brittleness, security). (2) The tool-call alternative, and why games are the perfect proof. (3) A tiny tool-call game engine: 12 functions, an agent NPC that plays by the rules. (4) Porting the idea to back-office software. (5) The security dividend (CaMeLs' argument, made concrete).
- **Non-obvious because:** it uses a game as a rigorous argument about enterprise software design — and takes a side in the computer-use vs. tool-call race instead of "both have merits."
- **Demo/project tie-in:** Tool-call RPG micro-engine (from the original notes): a grid game where every mechanic is a function; one agent plays it. Small, screenshottable, weird enough to share.
- **Research references:** Evolution of Tool Use (single call → orchestration trajectory); Verifiable Software Worlds (the theoretical frame); WebXSkill (skill learning as the competing approach); CaMeLs (security payoff).
- **Compare/cite:** Browserbase/Cua (adapt-to-UI camp) vs. Composio/Mastra (tool-registry camp); Pydantic as the quiet load-bearing layer for typed actions.

## Angle: Voice & realtime agents

### 10. The Voice Agent Latency Budget: Where Your 800 Milliseconds Go
- **Core thesis:** Voice agents live or die on a latency budget — roughly 800ms mouth-to-ear before conversation feels broken — and every architectural choice (RAG, tool calls, turn-taking) is a withdrawal from it. The recent research wave is all, secretly, about the same thing: buying back milliseconds.
- **Sources:** Daily (Pipecat), LiveKit, Vapi (voice platforms), Deepgram + AssemblyAI (streaming STT/TTS), Telnyx + Twilio + Plivo (telephony), Hamming AI (voice evals); papers: VoiceAgentRAG (dual-agent RAG to hide retrieval latency), LTS-VoiceAgent, Adaptive Turn-Taking, Building Interactive Real-Time Agents with Asynchronous I/O and Speculative Tool Calling, τ-Voice, ProVoice-Bench.
- **Why now:** Speech-to-speech models and full-duplex agents moved the bar from "impressive demo" to "usable phone line," and the papers now quantify what practitioners were tuning blind.
- **Practical angle:** An itemized latency budget table — STT, LLM first-token, retrieval, tool calls, TTS, network — with the paper-backed trick for each line item (speculative tool calling, dual-agent RAG, filler-aware turn-taking), and which platforms handle which for you.
- **Outline:** (1) Why 3 seconds of silence kills a call. (2) The budget, itemized. (3) The tricks, one per line item, each anchored to a paper. (4) Build: a phone line for job-status checks (caller asks, agent reads the packet auditor's state back). (5) Evaluating it: what τ-Voice and Hamming-style evals actually catch.
- **Non-obvious because:** it reads five separate papers as one engineering story (the budget), turning a literature pile into a debugging checklist.
- **Demo/project tie-in:** Vapi or Pipecat phone agent fronting the job packet auditor — "call the office line, ask if the Johnson job is ready." Connects the voice thread to the back-office thread in one demo.
- **Research references:** VoiceAgentRAG (retrieval off the hot path); Speculative Tool Calling (start the call before the sentence ends); Adaptive Turn-Taking (when to speak); τ-Voice + ProVoice-Bench (how to score it).
- **Compare/cite:** Daily/Pipecat vs. LiveKit vs. Vapi on the axis "framework vs. infrastructure vs. managed platform"; Deepgram vs. AssemblyAI on streaming latency.
