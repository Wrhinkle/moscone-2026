# Adaptive Turn-Taking for Real-Time Multi-Party Voice Agents

- **Status:** Located — [arXiv:2606.13544](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13544), accepted to Interspeech 2026
- **Area:** voice-realtime
- **Authors / venue / date:** Soumyajit Mitra, Prabhat Pandey, Abhinav Jain, Shanmukha Sahith, K V Vijay Girish (Amazon, per Amazon Science listing). arXiv v1 2026-06-11, v3 2026-06-26.

## Problem
Voice agents that work fine in one-on-one calls fall apart in multi-party settings (meetings, group calls): they can't tell when they're being addressed versus when two humans are talking to each other, so they either interrupt constantly or go silent. Classic VAD/endpointing answers "did someone stop talking?" but not "is it *my* turn, given who I am in this conversation?"

## Approach
ModeratorLM: a streaming speech LLM (speech encoder + Qwen3-4B-Instruct backbone, joined by a trainable linear projection, LoRA-tuned with 13.4M trainable params) that makes turn-taking decisions itself — no separate VAD module. Audio arrives in chunks; per chunk the model emits either a control token + response or nothing. The key idea is **role conditioning**: the agent's assigned role (moderator, note-taker, etc.) conditions both when to speak and what to say. A "Think" variant adds chain-of-thought reasoning traces before each turn decision. Training data: **RolePlayConv**, ~75K synthetic multi-party conversations (3–6 speakers, 125 role descriptions, dialogues generated with Amazon Nova Pro, TTS via Zonos-v0.1), on top of alignment on 90K hours of speech and pretraining on AMI/Fisher.

## Key findings
- On NOTSOFAR-1 real meeting recordings, ModeratorLM-Think: precision 0.81, recall 0.74, false-positive interruption rate 0.01, reactive miss rate 0.02.
- On the RolePlayConv test set (unseen roles, dialogues generated by a different LLM, QwQ-32B): precision 0.79, recall 0.82, FP rate 0.03.
- Versus non-role-conditioned baselines: >40% better precision, >70% better recall. Moshi (dyadic full-duplex model) performed poorly in multi-speaker settings.
- Ablations: performance degrades substantially without text transcripts in context — the model leans heavily on ASR, not raw audio alone (read from paper, not inferred).

## Limitations & open questions
Half-duplex only — the authors defer simultaneous listen-while-speaking to future work. Heavy dependence on streaming transcription means real-world ASR errors and diarization mistakes propagate into turn decisions. Fixed-size chunking misses cues at realistic inference boundaries. Training data is synthetic (LLM-scripted, TTS-voiced); NOTSOFAR-1's "assistant" speaker is a relabeled human, not a deployed agent. No end-to-end latency numbers reported — a builder should ask what a 4B speech LLM per audio chunk costs on the response-time budget.

## Why builders should care
If you're building a meeting or group-call agent, "when to speak" is a modeling problem, not a VAD threshold — and giving the model an explicit role description is a cheap, prompt-level lever that dramatically cuts false interruptions. It also argues for keeping speaker-attributed transcripts in the agent's context rather than betting on audio-only end-to-end models today.
