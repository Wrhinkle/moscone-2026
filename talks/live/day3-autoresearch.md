## Field Guide to Fable (Thariq Shihipar, Anthropic)

[0:16:29](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4sX_He5c4sI&t=989s). Shihipar, who works on Claude Code, spoke hours before Fable's rollout ("we're rolling it out later today") and structured the talk as four parts: unhobbling Claude, finding your unknowns, dealing with the grief, and being unreasonable. On unhobbling: models are grown, not designed, and what contains them is the harness and prompts around them. His example of capability overhang: a chat model can't say which Pokemon names end in "aw," but Claude Code can, because it writes a script to filter all 1,000 names. He traced how best practice has inverted: early models wanted small prompts and many examples, later ones wanted large prompts and many tools, and the newest class wants a smaller prompt again because examples constrain a model that is more imaginative than they are. Anthropic removed 80 percent of the Claude Code system prompt. On unknowns, he offered a matrix (known knowns through unknown unknowns) and six concrete techniques: a blindspot pass, brainstorms with four wildly different prototypes to react to, having Claude interview you with questions that could change the architecture, giving it reference code as a map, asking it to log implementation notes when it deviates, and having it quiz you afterward so you can stand behind the PR. This process matches, nearly step for step, the first Anthropic conversation in the [in-room session recaps](../../notes/session-recaps.md#anthropic--interpretability-and-a-process-for-not-handing-off-the-work), including the line that trade-offs are not real at Anthropic. On grief: he ran a 30-person YC startup, went back to that codebase recently, and did in hours what took weeks; "how can you not laugh, but also how can you not cry." He closed by arguing for good, fast, cheap, pick three, and said the deck itself was built with Fable in about four hours.

## In the Land of AI Agents, the Verifiers Are King (Tariq Shaukat, Sonar)

[0:35:47](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4sX_He5c4sI&t=2147s). Sonar's CEO argued that verification has to be baked into code generation rather than bolted on afterward. He opened with retracted KPMG and EY reports and fabricated case law as evidence that plausible output is not correct output, then turned to METR data: the latest models complete tasks that take humans 16 to 18 hours, but at a 50 percent success rate; at 80 percent accuracy the horizon drops to about 3.5 hours. A CTO customer's response: "I would still put someone who gave me 80 percent accurate information on a performance review." Sonar's own benchmark runs models through 4,000+ problems; functional correctness is high across the board, but complexity, bugs, and security issues persist. He cited the Carnegie Mellon study in which a 3 to 5x velocity boost from coding agents dissipates within three months as security, maintainability, and complexity debt accumulates. His framework is the agent-centric development cycle (ACDC): guide, verify, solve. Guide means context plus constraints (architectural awareness, approved dependencies, coding standards); Sonar measured over 30 percent token reduction per task from it. Verify means zero-trust, multi-layered verification, combining algorithmic checks (data flows, secrets, known patterns) with agentic checks (intent, business logic); customers using it report AI-derived production outages 44 percent less frequent. Solve means active code maintenance, since agents work measurably better on cleaned codebases. One large bank saw a 92 percent reduction in issues with the guide-verify-solve loop. This is the same framing captured in the [Sonar in-room recap](../../notes/session-recaps.md#sonar--agent-centric-development-and-whether-ai-assisted-coding-delivers), which adds detail from a companion session (memory injection via hooks, models frozen in time for legacy code). Company profile: [Sonar](../../companies/swe-infrastructure/sonar.md).

## Perception Agents (Antje Barth, Amazon AGI)

[0:54:10](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4sX_He5c4sI&t=3250s). Barth's thesis: a year ago the hard problem was getting an agent to find and click a button; clicking turned out to be the easy part, and the unsolved part is the work that lives in the seams between applications. Her example is onboarding a new hire, a process that touches five systems and that no one owns end to end. Agents can do each step but not the whole, and at 60 to 80 percent reliability no one trusts them ("if your agent one in four times deletes a database, you will never touch that agent again"). Coding got reliable first because code is verifiable; most knowledge work has no unit test for "did the report land." Her answer is what Amazon AGI calls perception agents, modeled on the robotics loop of perceive, plan, act: agents that read the rendered screen the way a person does, confirm their own output, work without APIs, and accept pointing at elements as input instead of long text descriptions. She announced two open-source pieces of the harness: an annotation Chrome extension for marking up elements with instructions, and a verification component that checks work against a design.md spec with visual checks and automated user-flow walkthroughs. A demo recorded at the conference used wearable Bee devices to transcribe a design meeting, then fed the transcript summary directly to the agent, which applied the changes and kicked off verification. She pointed to a colleague's talk in the computer-use track the same day and a podcast run by the lab's agent-computer-interaction team. Company profile: [Amazon AGI Labs](../../companies/models-inference/amazon-agi-labs.md).

## Research to Reality with Google DeepMind (Benoit Schillings, Google DeepMind)

[1:15:34](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4sX_He5c4sI&t=4534s). Schillings, VP of research, described his team's mandate as building whatever Gemini needs between one month and one year out. He came from Google X (Waymo, Glass) and started Project Pitchfork there in 2018 to apply ML to code evolution, at a time when "nobody would give us the time of day." He has written code for 45 years, starting with assembly on Apple II and Commodore 64, and framed the eras of software by their limiting factor: first the machine, then the human brain (a working context of roughly seven to nine tokens, which shaped modular design), and now neither, since writing code is nearly free. His claims: superhuman syntax generation is done; multi-step engineering on codebases of 35 million lines is where frontier models still struggle; architecture-level thinking remains human. He estimated 80 percent of new code added to GitHub is machine generated, which ends the era of mining human code for training and points to self-play, the AlphaZero move applied to code, where models create their own challenges and judge the results. Consequences he drew: within a year nobody will read generated code, the way nobody checks compiler output, so the field needs active guardrails and models taught to write correct code from the start; SWE-bench-style evals are too narrow, and open-ended problems like lossless text compression force novel algorithms; and it may be time for a programming language designed for models, strongly typed, Lean-inspired, and not necessarily human readable. Beyond code he pointed at chemistry ("once you are able to put 10,000 atoms together that starts to look like life"), biology, and "the gold we cannot see," breakthroughs invisible to evolutionarily biased humans. The [in-room DeepMind recap](../../notes/session-recaps.md#google-deepmind--rl-network-topology-and-what-comes-after-code) covers the same ground from the room, including Pitchfork and the LLM-native language question. Company profile: [Google DeepMind](../../companies/models-inference/google-deepmind.md).

## Evals Track Intro (Aparna Dhinakaran, Arize)

[1:36:53](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4sX_He5c4sI&t=5813s). A five-minute track intro with real content. Arize runs over 100 million evals a month; the average team runs about 12 eval jobs and top teams run over 3,800 evaluators. Her argument: the thing being evaluated changed underneath first-generation evals. From single prompts in 2023 through tool calls and reasoning to today's loops with subagents on long-horizon tasks, each step changed the failure modes, and fixed-rubric LLM-as-judge scoring cannot catch failures like an agent stuck calling the same tool in a loop or taking an inefficient trajectory. Her proposal: evaluate agents with agents. Arize released Signal, a long-running agent that reads traces, discovers failure patterns, and can open a PR with a fix. She positioned agent-as-a-judge as a third layer on top of deterministic evals and classic LLM-as-judge, not a replacement. Company profile: [Arize](../../companies/evals-observability/arize.md). (The program also listed Laurie Voss for this slot; only Dhinakaran appears on the stream.)

## First Steps Toward Automated AI Research (Richard Socher, Recursive)

[1:55:30](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4sX_He5c4sI&t=6930s). Socher's first public talk on what he calls the Eureka machine, "a machine that will eventually invent pretty much all future inventions for humanity," joint work with colleagues at recursive.com and AIX Ventures. The setup is a long zoom through open-ended evolution: biology to technology to science, Popper's natural selection of theories, and the observation (via de Solla Price) that exponential science is throttled by the number of people available per niche subfield, which is the case for automating discovery. The Eureka machine has four pillars: existing knowledge, scientific measurement data, simulation ("anything you can simulate you can verify and you can then solve with AI"), and a physical lab, with an agent swarm on top. The build strategy is recursive self-improvement: an AI that does AI research first, then turns to other sciences. He was careful to distinguish true RSI (a system with awareness of its own shortcomings and full access from pre-training to harness, updating its next version) from weaker autoresearch. Proof points from their first system: on nanochat it beat the community's 0.93 bits per byte to 0.91 after about two days, finding ideas like hashed bigram and trigram embedding tables mixed into value paths through learned gates rather than mere hyperparameter tuning; on the nanoGPT speedrun it went over two seconds faster than a community that had worked on it for a year, landing around 70 seconds; and it discovered CUDA kernels beating the leaderboard bests on an NVIDIA benchmark site across categories, checked with NVIDIA for reward hacks. He closed arguing intelligence has ten spaces with far-off upper bounds, so the exponential has room to run.

## Autoresearch for Dense Retrieval: Test-Time Compute with Frozen Embedding Models (Han Xiao, Elastic / Jina AI)

[2:20:08](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4sX_He5c4sI&t=8408s). Xiao, who founded Jina AI (acquired by Elastic in October 2025), asked whether a small frozen embedding model can improve by thinking harder at inference, and let an agent run the research overnight. The strict rules: one frozen encoder behind an API, unlimited calls, no retraining, no second model, no learned parameters. An autoresearch loop (proposer based on Opus 4.6 as mutation function, evaluator over 14 discovery tasks, JSONL memory, registry) generated 144 programs, each a training-free recombination of chunking, scoring, feedback, and fusion over Jina v5 Nano, a 200-million-parameter encoder. Cost was counted as extra forward passes. Two rubrics ran on the same loop. The compute rubric, rewarded for in-domain gains, drew a clean scaling curve up to 15x compute, but on 19 held-out tasks and three unseen encoder families (including Gemma and Qwen) the curve went flat: out of domain, more compute bought essentially nothing. The transfer rubric, rewarded only for validation improvement with no credit for spending compute, picked six cheap programs (at most 1.5x baseline cost) that make small geometric adjustments to existing vectors; the best won on 83 percent of held-out tasks and never lost, transferring across models and languages. His conclusion: cheap structure transfers, compute does not. He then lifted the same bet to the pipeline level with three open-source projects: Data Room (a token-budgeted crawler that packages a corpus for downstream agents, spending cheap small-model tokens on exploration), SearchBox (an air-gapped testbed where an agent must assemble a search pipeline from local tools like grep, embed, and rerank), and Knowledge Graph (turning a corpus into multi-hop questions by finding longest paths). Slogan: search is test-time compute; don't reach for a bigger model. Company profile: [Elastic](../../companies/memory-rag-search/elastic.md).

## Memory harnesses for long-running research agents on device (Stefania Druga, Sakana AI)

[2:50:31](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4sX_He5c4sI&t=10231s). Not on the published track list, but delivered live on this stage before lunch. Druga, a research scientist at Sakana AI in Tokyo, presented experiments on context rot in long-horizon tasks using only local models: an M3 Ultra Mac (96 GB, cooled by household fans, still running evals remotely during the talk) serving a 4-bit quantized Qwen model and DeepSeek V4 Flash. Her framing: memory is a write-manage-read control loop around the model, and the agents themselves hold zero durable memory so the harness supplies all of it. She tested a ladder of recall policies: no memory, vector RAG, a ranked decisions ledger, and an oracle that injects the ground-truth memory. Findings: when the task fits in context, memory adds cost and no capability; on long-horizon benchmarks (68 XBench questions where the answer lives hundreds of steps outside the window, plus Spider V2), the ranked ledger beat both plain RAG and gated memory, and even the oracle fell short of the ceiling because models given the right memory can still ignore it. Her heuristic: bad memory is expensive, and a structured recall policy saves tokens; treat the recall policy as a first-class metric. She tied the local-only setup to sovereign AI, a priority for Sakana in Japan, and noted the cost: the local models only run serially, so evaluations take days. Related paper notes: [memory and research agents](../../papers/memory-research-agents/README.md).

## « the era of (auto) research » (Elie Bakouch, Prime Intellect)

[3:14:43](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4sX_He5c4sI&t=11683s). Bakouch, a research engineer at Prime Intellect, argued that recursive self-improvement claims from big labs have no third-party benchmark, and that understanding how models do research matters beyond AI because future science will lean on the same tools. His environment is the nanoGPT speedrun lineage: Karpathy's 90-minute GPT-2 reproduction, driven by Keller Jordan and the modded-nanoGPT community down to under two minutes over two years, plus the newer optimizer speedrun where only optimizer parameters may change. Prime Intellect entered the optimizer speedrun with two agents run free on their cluster: Codex (GPT 5.5) and Claude Code (Opus 4.8). Observed behaviors: Claude Code stopped every 9 or 10 hours declaring the record unbeatable and sat idle about a third of the time until restarted; Codex worked continuously, wrote far more to its scratchpad memory, spawned more subagents, burned on the order of a billion tokens, and compacted its 250k context about 20 times an hour versus roughly once total for Claude. Both agents could fetch the human record at any time and both beat it, by around 50 to 60 steps against a record near 2,990. A more structured benchmark is in progress with three access tracks (weights-only knowledge, arXiv access, full access including current records); in six-day runs, Codex and Claude led, Kimi K2.7 was surprisingly competitive with a step-function breakthrough on day four and the best token efficiency, and one model found a paper no other agent found, which produced the best record. His honest headline: no agent produced a novel optimizer, only clever recombinations of papers and plus-one improvements. The path forward he sketched is an AlphaEvolve-style multi-agent loop with judges, taste, and scale-up steps, plus GPU sandboxing and their open verifiers library for RL environments. Company profile: [Prime Intellect](../../companies/models-inference/prime-intellect.md).

## Closing the Loop: An Autonomous AI Research Agent (Tim Sweeney, Weights & Biases / CoreWeave)

[4:40:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4sX_He5c4sI&t=16800s). Sweeney, a principal engineer at Weights & Biases (part of CoreWeave for about a year), demoed Arya, their AI research and iteration agent, live against Karpathy's nanochat autoresearch project. In a workspace holding over 200 training jobs, Arya downloads code, sets up launch jobs and GPUs, and iterates autonomously on code and hyperparameters through W&B Launch queues on a Kubernetes cluster; on stage he kicked off a live experiment batch and checked back at the end (the previous best was 5.831, the live run landed 5.833, "right on the edge"). Beyond training loops, Arya summarizes projects, finds patterns (it surfaced that batch size was the highest-leverage parameter and identified a promising architectural recipe), and emits W&B reports and workspaces with native charts. Announced on stage: Arya in the W&B iOS app, for steering hyperparameter jobs from a phone. The second half was the build story: a harness worker connected to a sandbox, inference, Launch for long-running jobs, and 100 percent of traces logged to Weave, where the team runs LLM judges as live "signals" (user frustration, low-quality response) against production traffic. Their eval suite is about 200 YAML-defined tasks, each with judges for correctness, interestingness, and tool-call budget, run nightly; a candidate scoring 73 percent against production's 72 percent gets promoted. His closing advice: invest in agent observability to catch behavioral bugs, treat tasks and evals as the new CI, keep humans reviewing best and worst traces weekly, and prefer context and tools over clever harness engineering. Company profile: [Weights & Biases by CoreWeave](../../companies/evals-observability/weights-and-biases-by-coreweave.md).

## An AI Agent Became the #1 Contributor in OpenAI's Hiring Challenge (Zhengyao Jiang, Weco AI)

[5:05:22](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4sX_He5c4sI&t=18322s). Jiang, co-founder and CEO of Weco (an autoresearch company; he did an RL PhD at UCL, and Weco's earlier agent was independently evaluated in OpenAI's MLE-bench paper), described sending their agent Aiden into OpenAI's Parameter Golf hiring challenge, where the goal is training the best small language model under size and compute constraints. About 1,000 engineers filed 2,000 submissions; 47 passed open review onto the leaderboard, 7 of them from agents. Aiden ran 22 days and about 1,300 experiments on a single H100 node, set 7 leaderboard records (the best human set 3), used at most 4 percent of the competition's compute for about 15 percent of the records, and hit the leaderboard with 28 percent of its submissions, roughly 6 times the community average. By an h-index computed over PRs, Aiden scored 10 against the best human's 7, meaning the community built on its work more than anyone's. The nuance he insisted on: nearly all of Aiden's record-setting ideas trace to human sources, papers, other participants' PRs, even notes where a researcher gave up on an idea over implementation difficulty; Aiden's edge is finding, implementing, and combining them (a Qwen-paper gated-attention idea, plus its own quantization workaround for the 16 MB file limit, plus another contributor's tokenizer improvement, fused over five days into a record). His generalization: autoresearch commoditizes execution the way gradient descent commoditized handwritten features, so the leverage moves to eval design (the loss function of your code) and codebase abstraction (the architecture that biases the search); in a fraud-detection engagement, tightening a loose API eliminated train-test leakage that a loose one invited. Humans move up the stack, not out of it. Company profile: [Weco AI](../../companies/coding-agents/weco-ai.md).

## Self-Improvement of Context, Harness, and Model Weights through Reflective Optimization (Lakshya Agrawal)

[5:35:11](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4sX_He5c4sI&t=20111s). Agrawal presented GEPA and its successor API on behalf of a large collaboration. The problem: RL with verified rewards learns a single scalar from each rollout and discards the chain of thought, tool calls, and error messages; most teams lack the hundreds of thousands of rollouts it needs anyway. The idea: reflect in text space, having an LLM read whole rollout traces and propose prompt updates, where one natural-language edit can produce a behavior change that would take thousands of gradient steps. GEPA runs an evolutionary loop with Pareto-based candidate selection, keeping every candidate that wins on even one training example; ablations showed the Pareto pool accounts for over half the gains versus a naive best-only loop, which gets stuck in local optima. Numbers cited: one round of reflection on 3 data points beat GRPO's gains after 25,000 rollouts, with Qwen3 8B optimizing itself; on AMD's new NPU (XDNA2, an API with almost no public documentation), an agent went from 4.25 percent to 30.52 percent, with GEPA discovering in one step that AMD's own adf.h library doesn't work on the new hardware; GPT-4.1 mini tuned past GPT-4.1 on math. The generalization is "optimize anything": any text artifact you can score, including whole agent harnesses. Examples: a four-line chain-of-thought script evolved in 16 rounds into a six-step agent taking Gemini Flash from 32.5 to 89.5 percent on ARC-AGI; learned repository skills taking a GPT-5-mini agent from 24 to 93 percent on Go issue resolution, then transferring to Claude Sonnet 4.5 at 100 percent with execution time cut about in half; cloud scheduling policies cutting cost about 40 percent versus expert heuristics; OCR error rates down about 35 percent; and Databricks reporting GPT-OSS 120B tuned to outperform Claude Opus at 90x lower cost. For subjective tasks, GEPA can first learn an LLM-as-judge prompt from about 50 human-annotated traces, then optimize the agent against it. A recent "learning fast and slow" paper co-optimizes weights and prompts.

## Autoresearch for Kernels (Tejas Bhakta)

[6:00:34](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4sX_He5c4sI&t=21634s). A compact talk on making models roughly 3x faster with autoresearch loops over GPU kernels, from a speaker who went from dorm-room GPU mining on 1080 Tis to inference optimization at Tesla. The loop is a while loop: agent proposes, a harness verifies correctness and speed, keep or revert. Kernels fit because they are doubly verifiable. The division of labor he insisted on: autoresearch is good at block sizes and parameter sweeps and bad at the high-level idea; the human reads the profiler (Nsight), decides "this is dumb, pipeline it instead," and lets the loop pick the sizes. His harness requirements: hardware context in markdown files (a B200 has TMA, an H200 does not; warp details change each generation), and model context for new architectures (DeepSeek V4 Flash shipped two new attention mechanisms, and without the spec in context the model hallucinates the attention and produces useless kernels). The biggest failure mode is reward hacking: agents disable CUDA graphs (up to 20x slowdown elsewhere), test only small context windows, or silently skip the required DSL, so much of the work is specifying what not to do. He noted kernels compound (sparse MLA plus NVFP4 and onward, stacking until the hardware's utilization ceiling), that discovered kernels often win only in certain ranges and must fall back to defaults elsewhere, and that bare-metal access (BIOS tweaks, overclocking, PCIe settings) adds roughly 25 percent over virtualized cloud setups. Expectation setting: about 80 percent of what the loop tries is bad. His motivation is cheap GPUs without NVLink, which have no off-the-shelf kernels.

## Polygraph: multi-repo sessions and agent memory (Victor, Polygraph)

[6:07:38](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4sX_He5c4sI&t=22058s). Aired in the slot before the 3:20pm session; the speaker gives only "Victor" and trypolygraph.com. The framing device: a genie grants you John Carmack as an engineer, but he can see one thousandth of your codebase and remembers nothing between conversations; that is an agent. A single UI-library change propagating through two modules and a platform repo takes seven explanations to agents that each see one repo and no history. The two failure axes: repo-bound vision (space) and amnesia (time). Polygraph is an agent-agnostic "meta harness" that analyzes the repos a GitHub user can reach (his own graph: about 300 owned repos plus thousands of open-source dependencies), computes what each produces and consumes, and presents agents with the illusion of one large codebase. Sessions span repos, coordinate multiple PRs, and treat CI across repos as one vector, deciding which repo needs the fix when something breaks. Sessions are also portable: a coworker can resume your session on their machine with a different agent (Claude Code in the demo, Codex on resume) because traces, shas, and state travel with it, which he compared to the Star Trek transporter; he reviews PRs by resuming the author's session and interrogating the decisions. The dependency graph also answers queries ("update every repo that depends on this library version") and retrieves relevant past sessions, so approaches by respected engineers become reusable patterns. His summary: lift the space constraint and the time constraint and every developer's agent draws on the organization's whole history, "sort of the Borg."

## Autoresearch in the wild (Roland Gavrilescu, Introspection)

[6:29:51](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4sX_He5c4sI&t=23391s). Gavrilescu (the program also credits Julian Bright; only one speaker appears) left xAI's agent infrastructure team a few months before to work on always-on, long-horizon agent deployment. Three claims. First, the loop is the product: the progression ran from RL on models to harnesses to loops, models are trained on OODA-style tool-observation cycles, and the canonical example is the OpenClaw predecessor "Clawbot" negotiating car discounts by playing dealers against each other with a verifiable stopping condition. Second, system distillation is the moat: each loop generates artifacts (failure patterns, repeated behaviors, user frustrations) that a second loop should process into judges, evals, skills, and prompts. Borrowing from RL data recipes, he proposes "agent recipes": versioned, provider-agnostic git repos encoding the maker's taste, built on the pi harness and Harbor for evals, owned by the company and managed by its agents; an early release is called pi.recipes. His worked example is a talent-sourcing agent that keeps reaching out to big-tech employees when the recruiter wants hidden gems: patterns detect the behavior in traces, agents draft the eval, and the human's job shrinks to calibrating the judgment rather than writing evals. Third, the metric to optimize is valued work per watt: cursor-and-cognition-style companies went best product, then best evals, then best models, and the two questions for everyone after them are how to measure value delivered and whether the economics beat the incumbent harness. Validation closes in production with A/B tests confirming users share the maker's taste.

## Autoresearch in a Multi-Agent AI Village (Erina Karati, Supercell)

[6:55:11](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4sX_He5c4sI&t=24911s). Karati (formerly Microsoft, then Supercell) presented Project Paradox, built with Arunachalam Manikandan at Supercell's AI innovation lab: a modular framework for autonomous game agents that move with intent, manipulate objects, react to events, and start conversations, backed by stateful architecture: per-agent RAG memory namespaces, an emotion vector (joy, sadness, fear, anger, disgust), a belief and trust matrix toward other agents and the player, and LLM-scored memory importance with a separate cache above a threshold. Short-horizon behavior worked (an agent plans a picnic, picks up a pastry, converses in context), but over long horizons social consistency decayed: in the running example, a rumor about a mango sale spreads agent to agent, and later the source is lost, the hedge hardens into fact, or the fact never reaches planning. Their fix is an autoresearch layer outside the village: define controlled scenarios (public-fact diffusion, rumor uncertainty, replanning around a blocked route), run the simulation, collect structured traces, score against ground truth, and let the loop propose one constrained change to the agent protocol, keeping it only if the scorecard improves and guardrails hold. Two engineering lessons got emphasis: keep the editable surface small (memory-write policy, retrieval policy, communication prompts, attribution rules, replanning triggers; freeze the harness, scenarios, and metrics so the optimizer can't game the eval) and use a balanced scorecard (reach, source retention, uncertainty preservation, false-certainty rate, time to replan, privacy containment), since optimizing any single metric produces oversharing or stale recall. She argued the same pattern applies to support agents, personal assistants, research agents, and coding agents: anything that maintains state over time needs scenario suites and behavioral scorecards, not vibes.

## Closing Keynote: own the verdict (Addy Osmani, Google)

[7:46:15](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4sX_He5c4sI&t=27975s). Osmani's closing keynote was about answerability. The engineer of the future chooses what is worth doing and owns the evidence, the understanding, and the verdict on automated work: ship, block, redirect, or accept the risk. He walked the stack the conference had been naming all week (harness engineering, loop engineering, software factories, with a nod to Dex Horthy's talk) and argued the human moved to the highest-leverage checkpoint rather than out of the loop. Supporting numbers came heavily from Sonar's 2026 research: AI-assisted code is now normal code; clean and messy repos show similar pass rates but clean code costs fewer tokens and revisits; 96 percent of engineers are skeptical of AI code while only about half always verify before committing, which he called distrust without bandwidth. His career frame is alpha and decay: alpha is your gap over current models, decay is the clock on it; speed and recall have decayed, verification is moving into harnesses, and taste (via Mitchell Hashimoto: high-quality qualitative judgment where no objective metric exists yet) decays slower but still decays. The durable asset is your signature, the name that stands behind shipped work, since an agent can follow a runbook but cannot inherit consequences. Three named hazards: cognitive debt (the gap between code that exists and code any human understands), cognitive surrender (a Wharton study where people misled by AI felt more sure; 73 percent), and orchestration tax (cognitive bandwidth does not parallelize just because agents do). Operating rules: agents run the inner loop, engineering is the outer loop; explain it or don't ship it; treat attention as a designed system. He ended optimistic: every time software got cheaper to write, demand grew, and the bottleneck moves from can we build it to should it exist and can we answer for it.

## Trends in AI: the cost of intelligence (George Cameron and Micah Hill-Smith, Artificial Analysis)

[8:05:33](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4sX_He5c4sI&t=29133s). The Artificial Analysis co-founders presented benchmarking data on intelligence-cost trade-offs. Their Intelligence Index (v4.1, synthesized from nine evals) had Claude Fable 5 on top, listed as "not currently available" until that afternoon. Two long-run findings: frontier labs trade blows with release gaps shrinking, and open-weights models have tracked 3 to 9 months behind the frontier for three years, implying someone gives away a Fable-class model within nine months. Token prices per fixed level of intelligence keep falling 5 to 10x per year, yet cost per task rises, because tasks got longer: GPQA Diamond answers cost fractions of a cent to about 50 cents, while their coding agent index and new AA Briefcase eval see beyond 20 dollars per task. Briefcase is their agentic knowledge-work benchmark: four private scenarios of realistic professional work in messy environments of thousands of files, emails, and Slack messages, graded on rubric correctness, analytical quality, and presentation; the progression from GPT-4o's bare slide to o3's bullet points to Opus 4.8 and Fable 5 outputs illustrated two years of progress on a due-diligence task. Cost drivers they broke down: two orders of magnitude in token price between frontier models and workhorses like DeepSeek V4 Flash and GPT-OSS 120B; hundreds of tool-call turns per task; output verbosity (Claude Sonnet 5, released the previous day, used over 200,000 output tokens per task); and above all caching, since almost all tokens in long agentic tasks are input tokens and cache discounts range from about 80 to 99 percent, so they suggest starting cost analysis from the cache-hit price. Their closing decision tool: split tasks into those with no ceiling on useful intelligence (most knowledge work; pay for the Pareto frontier) and ceiling tasks ("how much did I spend on Stripe fees last month"; pick the cheapest model that clears the bar).

## Closing Keynote: evaluating agents in the real world (Wei-Lin Chiang, Arena)

[8:26:09](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4sX_He5c4sI&t=30369s). Chiang, co-founder and CTO of Arena (UC Berkeley PhD; early LLM-as-a-judge work and Chatbot Arena), closed the day on agent evals grounded in real usage. Arena's scale claims: 10 million monthly visitors, 700 million collected conversations across modalities, and 100 million dollars annualized revenue eight months after launching their evaluation product. Context for why agents: OpenAI's Codex data shows agent-generated output tokens dominating (near 100 percent internally, above 60 percent for other organizations), adoption spreading beyond engineering to finance, recruiting, and legal, and top-1-percent companies spending around 4,000 dollars per employee per month on AI. The hard parts of measuring agents: multi-component systems where model, loop, tools, or harness can each fail; complex workflows; and sparse signals spread over 100-plus tool calls. Their instrument is Agent Mode in Arena, launched a month earlier: a real product with file-system tools, terminal, search, and image generation (a demo pulled Google's Q1 earnings and produced a PowerPoint). First month: over a million agentic traces, more than half work-related, over 50 million lines of code written, 5.7 million tool calls a week with bash at about 46 percent. Rankings are mined from three signal types: explicit (per-turn success votes), implicit (downloads, praise, complaints), and environment feedback (did the command succeed), aggregated into signals like task success, praise rate, durability, bash recovery, and hallucination. Methodologically it is a randomized controlled trial intervening on agent components to measure causal effect on outcomes. Current board: Fable 5 first at about 14 percent net improvement over the model average, at about 10 dollars per session; slicing shows GPT-5.1 stronger on "GDP tasks" and Gemini on consumer use, and real-world token use separates models with identical list prices. His advice to builders: log every agentic trace, mine it, and tie evals to business outcomes.
