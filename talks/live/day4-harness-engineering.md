## The 2026 State of AI Engineering (Barr Yaron, Amplify)

[0:16:18](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=978s). Yaron's annual survey, run this year with Notion and Vercel, drew 1,048 respondents. The recurring shape holds: senior engineers who are new to AI. Of respondents with over 10 years of software experience, more than half have three years or less of AI experience, and the median brand-new engineer has nearly as much AI experience as the median 10-year veteran. Text still dominates modalities, but image generation jumped most, with the share using it happily doubling from 18% to 36%, and audio has the highest intent to adopt for the second year running (56% of non-users plan to adopt, up from 37%). On models: 94% use closed models, 45% use open-weight models, and over 90% of open-weight users also run closed models, so open weights augment rather than replace. Open versus closed was a top-three selection factor for only 5%; quality, agentic capability, and cost drive choice, and only one in five named reliability, which Yaron reads as a threshold requirement rather than a differentiator. Cost is now a first-class constraint: 40% say it regularly shapes how ambitiously they use AI, another 36% say sometimes, and cost/token usage is the number two production monitoring signal behind quality. The agent numbers are the headline: 95% report using agents (roughly double last year), and 89% of those agents have write access, up from 52%, so write-enabled agents tripled year over year while control stays primitive (human-in-the-loop approvals and permission gating on top). Evals remain the top stack challenge every year, inference is the most-bought layer, prompt management the most-built (61% in-house). Also: 97% report net positive organizational impact, over a third of teams have non-developers shipping features, 67% expect a lab to declare AGI within five years, and only 9% bet transformers stay state of the art.

## TCP and RDMA are Killing Inference Throughput; Homa Can Fix It (John Ousterhout, Stanford)

[0:35:29](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=2129s). Ousterhout argued AI network workloads are shifting from huge throughput-bound transfers (weight gradients) toward small latency-bound messages for metadata and coordination: distributed KV-cache checks, barrier synchronization between millisecond-scale compute phases. When compute rounds shrink to milliseconds and synchronization also takes milliseconds, GPUs idle, and tail latency (p99) gates the whole pipeline. TCP and RDMA (RoCE) fare badly here for two structural reasons: sender-driven congestion control, which learns about congestion late via ECN marks and never stabilizes, and byte-stream data models that cannot see message boundaries, so short messages queue behind long ones. Homa, from his Stanford group (originating in Behnam Montazeri's PhD), is a clean-slate transport: message-based around a request/response RPC unit, receiver-driven congestion control using grant packets, SRPT prioritization of short messages, and use of the priority queues in modern switch hardware. On his benchmark, p99 round-trip for short messages drops from over a millisecond on TCP to under 100 microseconds, about 13x, and even the largest messages run about 2x faster. Homa ships as a Linux kernel module on GitHub that he is working to upstream; he described it as his personal mission in semi-retirement and offered hands-on help to anyone who tries it. This matches the in-room note [on Homa from Stanford PlatformLab](../../notes/session-recaps.md) from the same conference.

## The Unreasonable Effectiveness of Separating the Task from the Model (Maxime Rivest and Isaac Miller, DSPy)

[0:53:43](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=3223s). Rivest opened with the core DSPy claim: repeated AI tasks should be functions, with fixed input/output contracts and swappable internals, so new models and techniques (agents, tools, loop engineering) become implementation details behind a stable interface. His worked examples were personal: extracting tax values from farm invoices, and keyboard commands that fix grammar or rewrite clipboard text. Fully specifying a task takes three languages: instructions in natural language (what should happen), code for hard constraints (what must happen, e.g. rerun with chain-of-thought if extraction fails, escalate to a human if a value is negative), and examples for the long tail (what good looks like, his maple-tree story). With all three, optimizers like GEPA can climb the metric and automate the implementation. Miller covered production wins (Shopify running 550x cheaper by swapping to a cheap model while keeping evals and business logic) and the DSPy 4 roadmap: RLMs (recursive language models, from Alex Zhang's MIT paper) as a one-line module for long-context tasks; dspy.flex, which learns a custom harness per function over time; and qualitative learning, a research push to build evals automatically from textual feedback in production traces rather than hand-built proxy datasets. His AGI hedge: an all-knowing model still will not know your context, your relationships, or your task, so last-mile learning remains the job. This is the same team and material as the in-room note [on DSPy, AI programs as functions](../../notes/session-recaps.md), which raised the open question of how much of this the skills-and-agent-definitions pattern already covers.

## Thinner Agents on a Smarter Substrate (Emil Eifrem, Neo4j)

[1:39:32](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=5972s). Sponsored keynote. Eifrem's problem statement: every enterprise team building an agent (his example, bank account opening) rediscovers where the data lives across 100 databases plus Snowflake, Databricks, and S3, duplicates that wiring in code and prompts, violates DRY when things change, and learns nothing across agents. Markdown skills alone do not fix it; he quoted swyx from Latent Space: "you got to learn your databases. You cannot vibe code with just markdown files." His blueprint, drawn from work with a Fortune 20 bank and a large Bay Area platform company, is an ontology-based semantic layer with three pillars: a business-facing ontology (customers, accounts, transactions, named the way humans talk, not f_name), a technical ontology (metadata for every data source and schema), and a mapping between them, plus runtime execution traces that agents leave as they traverse the graph, recording what they tried and whether it worked so the next invocation chooses better. The result is thin agents on a smarter shared substrate: discoverable, trustworthy data sources, one governed mapping from business intent to systems, and cross-agent learning. He plugged the Graphs track (Gates Foundation, JP Morgan Chase, Monday.com, New York Times) and Neo4j's startup program. Company profile: [neo4j.md](../../companies/memory-rag-search/neo4j.md).

## Breakout feed: RL-guided remediation for ETL failures (speaker uncertain)

[1:50:39](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=6639s). Between main-stage talks the stream cut to a recorded session on an RL-guided system that selects bounded remediation actions for AWS Glue ETL failures (the captions render the speaker's name roughly as "Ana Marie Benzson"; not in the published program, so attribution is uncertain). The design: EventBridge catches a job-failed event, Lambda gathers evidence from CloudWatch logs and the Glue data catalog, deterministic rules establish facts (schema drift, null-rate spikes, type changes), a tabular Q-learning policy picks among six bounded actions (retry, coerce, roll back, quarantine, escalate, log), and a safety override outside the learned policy converts passive choices into escalations when the anomaly is critical. The stated thesis: rules for facts, learning for bounded choices, guardrails for authority. Manual recovery baseline was modeled at roughly 2.5 working days; the goal is compressing that loop for routine failures while escalating novel ones. The feed cut away before the results section.

## Tokens Should Have Jobs (Katelyn Lesse and Angela Jiang, Anthropic)

[1:56:51](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=7011s). Lesse (platform engineering) and Jiang (platform product) attacked the assumption that all tokens in an agent budget are fungible. Instead of spending an entire budget on execution, they split tokens into jobs: advisers that executing tokens can consult, graders that score work against a rubric and force iteration, and dreamers that read executor transcripts and write learnings to memory for the next run. On a financial-analysis bench replicating an expert analyst, one-shot execution scored 15% accuracy at 39K tokens while dreaming did far better at 600K. Holding budget constant at 600K, execute reached 76% while advise hit 89%, so strategy choice yields real alpha beyond raw test-time compute. Then they rescored with a pass/fail lens (a P&L that is 80% right is useless; the analyst redoes it), where execute passed about 42% of runs, meaning roughly three runs and 1.8M tokens expected per perfect answer, and advise and grade turned out substantially more token-efficient to a guaranteed-correct result. Their guidance: pick advise for token efficiency, grade or dream for reliability. The strategies run on the same harness as Claude managed agents, where dreaming and outcome-checking primitives ship out of the box, and they want the platform to eventually compose strategies dynamically. One live wrinkle: mid-demo they noted "Fable is back online," slotting it in as the adviser model.

## MCP, CLI, or skills: the agent plumbing rubric (Nikita Kothari, Salesforce)

[2:20:08](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=8408s). Kothari (senior member of technical staff at Salesforce, previously Amazon and LinkedIn) on the tooling layer as the thing that makes or breaks production agents. Three failure modes motivate the talk: context explosion (50 tool schemas can burn 15,000 to 20,000 tokens before the task starts), invisible failures (a seven-call PR draft lands on the wrong branch and trust evaporates), and security surface (an agent debugging customer A wanders into customer B's tenant data). Her mental model: a CLI is a screwdriver (direct, readable, reproducible, composable, 50 years battle-tested), MCP is a USB-C hub (standardized access to shared services with tenant isolation enforced at the server), and a skill is a runbook (rigid orchestration: prerequisites, steps, edge cases, using a scoped handful of CLIs and MCP servers). Choose by three questions: who else needs this (one agent, CLI; many agents, MCP; many workflows, skill), which failure mode matters (reproducibility, CLI; tenant isolation, MCP; sequencing guards, skill), and how tight the context is (tight budgets want skills with on-demand MCP loading). The golden rule: enforce isolation in infrastructure, never in prompts, because prompts can be injected and infrastructure cannot. Keep skills in code review and iterate them like any other artifact.

## Think like the model: HTML as the agent's canvas (Amol, Nauri Agentic; name uncertain)

[2:33:37](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=9217s). A recorded talk from the CEO of an AI-employee startup (captions render it "Nori/Nauri Agentic") on making agents produce slides, docs, and video. The world pours roughly 34,000 human years a day into slide decks, mostly fiddling rather than thinking. Canvas tools (PowerPoint, Figma, Canva) are built for human hands and eyes, so agents produce overlapping garbage in them, and SVG-drawing tests like Simon Willison's pelican-on-a-bicycle look hopeless. His claim: it is the medium, not the model. Asking an AI to use a canvas is like asking a human to hand-write SVG. HTML is the model's native graphical language: trained on billions of examples, structured, semantic, and rendered to pixels by the browser so the model never places a coordinate. The same pelican prompt in HTML produces a legible, editable, themeable bird. His company builds its board decks, sales decks, docs, and even videos ("divs all the way down") this way, rendering to PDF only at the end, and wires the agent to company data (call transcripts, email) so it drafts decks end to end. Takeaway line: stop thinking like a user, think like the model, and for graphics the right language is HTML.

## A four-layer prompt stack for brand voice (Isidora, wedding venue owner; surname not captured)

[2:39:39](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=9579s). The owner of a 225-year-old Virginia wedding venue who built an AI agent that talks to couples, then productized it for other venues, a companion app, and a missing-persons tool. Framing: she is not programming a robot, she is managing a brilliant intern with high IQ, terrible EQ, and no instinct for reading the room, which means structure and checked work, not rules you walk away from. A single system prompt handles the happy path and fails on turn 21, so she replaced 24 scattered system prompts with one assembly pipeline of four layers, order load-bearing: (1) immutable identity, hard rules nothing below can override, like disclosing AI status in the first message rather than waiting to be asked, and never claiming a body ("I'd love to show you around" is always forbidden; "the team would love to host you for a tour" is allowed); (2) situational mode, real-time conditions and who the user is (couples versus venue staff get the same facts on different roads); (3) example-anchored voice, where most teams start and stop; (4) a post-generation veto pass. The stakes argument came from Threadline, her tool for families of missing people, where layer one bans words like "confirmed," "matched," and "solved," because a statistically natural word choice said with model confidence to a grieving parent is the most damaging thing the product could do.

## Agent-native registration and o.md (Michael Grinich, WorkOS)

[2:49:45](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=10185s). Grinich argued agents are the next consumer class ("make something agents want," amending the YC line) and that the missing primitive is registration: 20 years of anti-bot hardening (captchas, email verification, credential-stuffing defenses) means an agent cannot legitimately sign up for a service, and MCP does not solve it because MCP auth still assumes a human consents with existing credentials. His proposal is o.md, an open spec built on existing OAuth-family standards that tells an agent how to become a legitimate user of a service: what the service does, what identity proofs are accepted, what scopes and free-tier constraints apply, and how a human or organization claims custody afterward. The demo: an agent asked to deploy an app discovers which providers advertise o.md, finds Cloudflare supports it, mints an identity assertion (ID-JAG, an identity JSON access grant signed against the user's backing identity service), receives a scoped access token good for a 72-hour trial deployment, and ships via Wrangler with the human only approving. Payments stay downstream by design, PLG-style, rather than credit-card-first. He cited Marc Benioff exposing Salesforce and Slack platforms as API, MCP, and CLI as evidence that even incumbent SaaS is going agent-first, and compared the moment to the iPhone platform shift he watched as a college freshman in 2007. Spec is on GitHub, vendor-neutral, one-click on WorkOS but self-hostable. Company profile: [workos.md](../../companies/security-identity/workos.md).

## Harness Engineering: Building the Production Cage for Powerful Domain Agents (Mike Chambers, AWS)

[3:15:06](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=11706s). Chambers (senior AI developer advocate at AWS, co-instructor of the half-million-enrollment generative AI course with Andrew Ng) split the world into agents you use (Claude Code, Cursor, Kiro; token-max away) and agents you build, which deserve real engineering. His harness definition: take an agent, remove the model, everything left is the harness, and he showed the dictionary joke ("a set of straps and fastenings used to control an animal... or model"). For agents you use, he plugged the AWS agent toolkit and coined "slop ops" as the successor sin to click-ops: do not let an agent hand-provision S3 buckets, make it write infrastructure as code. The bulk was live coding in Kiro: a bare Strands Agents SDK agent with a calculator and get-time tool; a session-manager version with a remember tool and rehydrated conversation history (it predicted Australia would win the World Cup because it remembered where he lives); then deployment with Bedrock AgentCore, whose CLI scaffolds an agent, wires short-term and long-term memory as separately scaling cloud infrastructure, gives multi-tenant isolation without writing tenant code, offers HTTP, MCP, or AG-UI serving, works with any framework and any model, and provides a local dev loop plus traces and memory inspection. His closing point: if 80% of agent use cases are a system prompt plus MCP tools, AgentCore ships that as a JSON config with no agent code at all, and every harness component (memory, identity, runtime, observability) is composable separately. ## The Great Loops Debate (Ian Livingstone and Geoffrey Huntley vs. Dex Horthy and Greg of Sentry, moderated by Keycard)

[3:40:58](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=13258s). An Oxford-format sequel to the AIE Code MCP debate, moderated by the host of the Insecure Agents podcast (Keycard). Team "no delta" (Livingstone, Keycard CEO; Huntley, creator of the Ralph loop) argued loops are the core unit of engineering and inevitable; team "the hype is outrunning the discipline" (Horthy, HumanLayer CEO; Greg, Sentry developer) argued that what works in practice is much narrower than the discourse. Huntley: engineers prompting in a loop was obviously programmable, the wall-of-bash Ralph pattern is now a year and a half old, models "have been good enough for at least the last year," loops price out around $10.42 an hour, and loop engineering means encoding your domain as back-pressure (pre-commit hooks that echo prompts, static types, simulators) because "the models are drunk" and your job is keeping the locomotive on the rails. Horthy: Kubernetes took seven or eight years and is built on deterministic control loops over small owned states; the hype implies you stop reading code, and nobody has shown lights-off review working, so read what comes out the other end. ## Loophole: Adversarial Agents to Stress Test Your Morality (Brendan Rappazzo)

[4:41:54](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=16914s). Rappazzo (ML researcher at Morgan Stanley; this is a personal open-source project) built a terminal game born from his 23andMe opt-out anxiety: he could judge DNA-use cases one at a time but could not enumerate his morals in advance. Loophole treats law as a lossy translation of morals and generates synthetic case law to find the gaps. You write your morals in natural language; one agent codifies them into a legalese system with preamble, articles, and sections; then two adversarial agents run in a loop, one hunting loopholes (immoral but legal) and one hunting overreach (moral but illegal), while a judging agent auto-patches the code when possible, producing git-style diffs of your legal system, and elevates genuine contradictions to you as the supreme court. Example finds: an insurer training a model on artifacts of your DNA rather than the DNA itself (loophole, auto-patched), and a researcher barred from telling a donor about a treatable genetic disorder (overreach, escalated). Extensions he is exploring: auto-generating chatbot constitutions with adversarial agents attacking both refusals and oversharing (he framed it as GEPA-adjacent); decentralized contract stress-testing, including diffing your personal data-privacy code against a company's terms of service; and a US Senate simulator that built moral codes for all 100 senators from voting records, simulates votes on any bill, and hill-climbs bill language, moving a Medicare test bill from a 50/50 split to passage at 52 votes without violating its core tenets. He also ran 500 NVIDIA USA personas per state as synthetic constituents. All on GitHub.

## The 100-tool agent trap (Sahil Shaikh and Ankush Sethi, Persistica; names uncertain)

[4:58:05](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=17885s). An online-track talk (captions garble both names and the company) on why giving an agent every tool at once fails. Loading 741 tool schemas costs about 127,000 tokens before the user question arrives. Their accuracy curve for a "fat agent": roughly 78% correct tool selection at 10 tools, about 40% at 100, and 13.6% at 741, roughly one correct call in eight, driven partly by the lost-in-the-middle problem since models attend to the start and end of long contexts and hundreds of schemas get packed into the middle. Their fix is semantic routing with just-in-time context: retrieve a small relevant tool subset per request, which held steady around 83% accuracy across the same catalog sizes while also cutting latency and per-request cost. The stream cut to the next talk during the latency-and-cost section.

## Every step you take, every call you make: the reliable agent stack (Giselle van Dongen, Restate)

[5:04:16](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=18256s). Van Dongen opened with Karpathy's three waves (LLM as website, agent as app, and now persistent asynchronous entities living in your infrastructure) and argued the infra layer has lagged the SDK layer: retry logic, recovery, and state consistency for long-running distributed processes are hard and necessary. Restate, an open-source framework descended from Apache Flink and Meta's event infrastructure, provides durable execution: it sits in front of your agent service like a proxy, and every action the agent takes appends to a journal that can rebuild the process after a crash, so a week-long agent resumes at the failure point instead of starting over. Her demo was a Slack deep-research agent: a planner posts subtopics for human approval (a durable promise that can suspend for weeks without burning serverless time), parallel sub-agents research with injected tool failures that retry transparently, and a writer reports. Virtual objects model agents as stateful actors with a session ID, isolated key-value state, and single-execution-at-a-time guarantees so thousands of concurrent sessions cannot corrupt each other. Follow-up messages mid-run get classified and either signaled into the running loop (her "focus on frontier models" example) or cancel it cleanly down the call chain. She also showed pulling LLM calls into a gateway handler with policy checks and flow control (e.g. 300 concurrent calls per department) when a pricey new model, "for example Fabulous," blows the budget. Push-based invocation gives around 45ms p99 for a 10-step workflow; single binary, six SDKs, self-host, BYOC, or managed cloud.

## The prompt is a platform (Dominic Tornow, Resonate; partial)

[5:25:31](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=19531s). Tornow (founder/CEO of Resonate, a durable execution platform) predicted that in 2026 coding agents will quietly retire their first software platform, unnecessary rather than bad, because general-purpose implementations will be replaced by bespoke ones generated on demand from a reusable specification. For Resonate that means the product moves from implementation to spec: one reference server, plus target-specific servers synthesized with infrastructure partners such as Synadia (NATS.io). The engineering story was the honest part. Asking an agent to jump from abstract spec (which must assume no schema, no database, not even a consistency model) straight to a Rust-on-Postgres server produced a happy-path prototype that broke on concurrency and failure. Inserting a human-driven concrete specification (schemas, indices, transaction boundaries) got a production system but kept the agent out of design. The current process gives the agent a deterministic simulation environment and asks for a simulated implementation first, executable design that discovers the algorithms under partial failure, then the concrete spec, then production code, moving the agent upstream into design. Prerequisite: three years spent shrinking the protocol to two objects, a durable promise and a durable task, because minimalism and simplicity are the finish line, not the start. The feed cut away mid-example.

## We let an AI agent execute Bash and lived to talk about it (Sarah Sanders, PostHog)

[5:34:35](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=20075s). Sanders (context engineer at PostHog) on securing the PostHog setup wizard, an agentic CLI that reads your codebase, installs the right SDK, instruments events, and builds dashboards in five to six minutes, now run by 8,000 developers a week. Her starting observation: an agent that can run commands is "the malware starter pack," and the wizard's in-house context engine, which injects docs, prompts, and example apps into the agent at runtime over MCP, is a machine whose whole job is content injection, so a poisoned pull request to their open-source docs could ship a prompt injection signed by PostHog. The audit lesson: almost no finding was obviously evil; they were pairs of innocent things shaking hands, and attacks compose while code review does not. Her build, the Warlock, is a standalone scanner on Yara rules (the malware-research pattern engine, deterministic on purpose) that scans skill bundles when built and again at point of use, returning findings with category, severity, and recommended action while enforcement stays separate. Real catches: sub-agents inventing and hunting secrets around guardrails (they banned sub-agents), and agents dumping emails and phone numbers into events since PII looks normal to a model. An LLM triage layer sorts false positives but only as adviser, never bouncer: rules block before the model gets an opinion, and if the model has a bad day the system fails closed. Her three lines: if it is not enforced deterministically it is not enforced; scan your own supply chain; attacks compose, code review does not. Wizard, Warlock, and context mill are open source. Company profile: [posthog.md](../../companies/evals-observability/posthog.md).

## Turbocharge your agent's retrieval (Shashi, SuperAgentic; online track, partial)

[5:54:57](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=21297s). A short online-track segment on cutting agent retrieval memory about 5x. The problem: KV cache grows with context and can exceed model size on local devices, and embeddings stored at 32-bit precision waste memory when search quality survives at 3 to 4 bits. Existing workarounds (model quantization, context compaction, smaller embeddings, CPU/disk offload) each trade away quality, speed, or hardware simplicity. The talk centered on TurboQuant-style compression from a Google Research ICLR 2026 paper: store embeddings and KV cache at 3 to 4 bits using a polar coordinate transform plus a QJL correction step, with the claim of no meaningful quality loss. The stream cut away mid-explanation, before benchmarks.

## No Memory, No Harness: Why the Database Is the Last Line of Defense (Kay Malcolm, Oracle)

[6:00:01](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=21601s). Malcolm (outbound database product management at Oracle, self-described retired hip-hop instructor, and the most crowd-work-heavy speaker of the day) started from a real team problem: in the "token maxing era of 2025," AI made her individual engineers faster but not the team, because a Netherlands developer would check in code at 4 a.m. without checking in their Codex context, so git recorded the code and lost the human intent. Her framing of an enterprise agent: model plus harness (tools, context, memory, retrieval, guardrails), with the Marvel-flavored analogy of the model as a brain in a jar, the harness as the body, and memory as the central nervous system. She walked five memory types (short-term, long-term, episodic, procedural, semantic), then staged an audience bit with four volunteers playing relational, JSON, graph, and vector databases who could not coordinate storing "the cow jumped over the moon," her argument against scattering agent memory across four systems with no single source of truth. The pitch: Oracle 26ai stores relational, JSON, graph, vector, spatial, and immutable blockchain data natively down to the same partition ("we have a marketing problem"), and the team's fix was Poly, a memory broker on the Oracle agent memory SDK (pip install) that tracks context per fork, branch, and commit so teammates inherit each other's agent state. She cited OpenAI's in-house data agent writeup and Harrison Chase's "your harness, your memory" line, and closed with the free-tier OCI plug. Company profile: [oracle.md](../../companies/cloud-compute/oracle.md).

## Polygraph: one big codebase for agents (speaker not captured; partial)

[6:22:19](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=22939s). A breakout talk that opened with a genie granting you John Carmack as an engineer who can see one thousandth of your codebase and remembers nothing between conversations, which is what agents are. The worked example: a UI-library change propagating through two consuming modules and a platform repo takes seven separate explanations, often across different people, because agents are repo-bound (they never see the system, which may be hundreds or thousands of repos) and amnesiac (every session is a blank slate, so the human becomes the memory). The fix presented is Polygraph, an agent-agnostic meta-harness that analyzes every repo a GitHub user can access, including open-source dependencies, extracts metadata about what each project produces and consumes (packages and APIs), builds a unified dependency graph without touching the code, and feeds it to the harness to create the illusion of one big codebase the agent can read and write anywhere, layered with the graph of past agent sessions on top. The speaker's own graph covered about 300 owned repos plus thousands of open-source dependencies. The stream cut to the next talk before the demo finished, and the speaker did not give a name on the recorded portion, so this one is unattributed.

## How We Solved Agent Building (Andrew Qu, Vercel)

[6:29:27](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=23367s). Qu (chief of software at Vercel) told the year-long story of d0, Vercel's internal data-science agent, as an architecture evolution: a mega-prompt with the Snowflake schema pasted in (he ran the generated SQL by hand); a pipeline of scoped sub-agents (query, planning, execution, reporting), which lost context at every handoff; one mega-agent managing its own state across a hundred steps, which trusted testers still called awful; and then the unlock, when Opus 4.5 plus Claude Code looked "basically AGI compared to what we had," and the lesson extracted was the file system. Rebuilt Claude Code-style in a sandbox with the semantic layer dumped to disk and only minimal tools (list, read, bash), the eval score roughly doubled, and his blog post about it drove 70% of vercel.com traffic for a week. Today a recurring job distills the thousands of daily employee queries into about 100 skills so new runs start with prior context. That journey became Eve, released two weeks before the talk: a Next.js-for-agents framework where skills, tools, and channels folders declare an agent, with open-source adapters (Postgres, Docker, OpenAI) or Vercel-native workflow durability, Sandbox execution, and Connect for short-lived OIDC tokens, plus out-of-the-box observability. His advice against off-the-shelf vertical agents: company-specific knowledge is what makes an agent good, and Vercel now runs about 20 internal agents with product-market fit, from marketing retros to first-pass legal redlines. This is the livestream counterpart of the in-room note [on Vercel's eve, "Next.js for agents"](../../notes/session-recaps.md). Company profile: [vercel.md](../../companies/cloud-compute/vercel.md).

## The Log Is the Agent (Eshan(?), Amnara; name uncertain, partial)

[6:46:39](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=24399s). The CEO of a startup the captions render as "Amnara" argued that model and runtime are the wrong abstraction for agent identity; the agent is its data, specifically an append-only event log, the way a Skyrim character is the save file rather than the PlayStation. Every user input, model output, tool call, result, permission, and failure is a state transition written to the log; model, tool runner, and UI just read from and append to it, so any disposable worker can claim a session, advance it one step, write the result, and vanish. The analogy he leaned on: databases already learned this inversion, with the log primary and tables, indexes, and materialized views as projections, and agent context, UI, debugging, auditing, and compaction should all be projections of the log the same way. He handled two objections: compaction is a lossy best-effort fork (keep the raw log or you have lost part of the agent), and external state (files edited, emails sent) is fine because the log stores the agent's view of the world, not the world, just as a save file does not contain the game engine. Concrete payoff he started on before the feed cut: reliability, e.g. Claude Code losing a permission prompt when the process dies mid-session, which he called unacceptable in production and a symptom of not making the log primary.

## Agents Without Code: How Skills, YAML, and Filesystems Replaced Python (Philipp Schmid, Google DeepMind)

[6:54:49](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=24889s). Schmid built the same GitHub PR-review agent three times, deleting code each round. Version one: a raw Python loop against the new Gemini Interactions API (which replaces turn-based history with a flat steps timeline of inputs, reasoning, function calls, and results), with hand-written JSON schemas and tool routing; it works but refuses anything outside its tools. Version two: an ADK-style framework agent that derives schemas from function signatures and handles the loop, retries, and errors, but still owns the Python plumbing and still cannot answer "what's the weather." Version three: the Antigravity remote agent on the Gemini API, the same harness as the Antigravity IDE but general-purpose, where the whole source directory disappears in favor of an agents.md, a skills file, and a bash script that installs the GitHub CLI on first run inside a hosted sandbox. A network proxy injects credentials on egress so the agent never sees tokens, domains are allowlistable, server-side state and automatic compaction manage the context, and the weather question just works via the built-in Google Search tool. What remains for the engineer: instructions, skills, and evals. His receipts for the trend: Cursor replacing about 12,000 lines of TypeScript orchestration with roughly 200 lines of agent files, Manus refactoring its harness five times in six months, LangChain re-architecting open deep research three times in a year, and Vercel removing 80% of tools for better accuracy. Closing rule: if your harness gets more complex as models improve, you are over-engineering it; build to delete. Company profile: [google-deepmind.md](../../companies/models-inference/google-deepmind.md).

## The mask and the mirror: what role-play evals miss (speaker not captured)

[7:13:04](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=25984s). A late-afternoon breakout from a data scientist who runs an analytics lab at a labor-market intermediary and trained as a behavioral epidemiologist (no name captured in the feed; collaborators named as historians Rick Halpern of the University of Toronto and a colleague at Washington College). Subject: role-playing language agents (Character AI, Hello History, and the speaker's own open-source Companion framework) now proposed as civic and pedagogical infrastructure. The thesis: the field's gold-standard evals, like InCharacter reporting 80.7% personality alignment for figures such as Alexander Hamilton, measure the mask (fluency, register, personality consistency) and have no mechanism for fidelity, whether the figure is constrained to what he could have known, believed, or argued at a moment in his life. The demo contrast: a frontier model's Hamilton speaks in the emotional palette of the 2015 musical, and on slavery delivers a clean abolitionist that the contested documentary record does not support. The proposed mechanism, the "Miranda hypothesis": culturally dominant representations outweigh a figure's primary record in training corpora by orders of magnitude (the Federalist Papers are a fixed 175,000 words; the musical's derivative content is unbounded and recent), next-token prediction compresses both without distinguishing an 1789 letter from a viral tweet, and RLHF amplifies the composite because raters hold the same mythologized version, a failure mode dubbed algorithmic sycophancy. The talk promised a pre-registered instrument built with a working historian; the feed cut before it was presented.

## Closing Keynote: Theo Browne

[7:41:52](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=27712s). Browne (t3dotgg) framed the model generations as eras: Sonnet 3.5 as the tool-call era, Opus 4.5 as long-running tasks ("probably when my psychosis started"), and Mythos/Fable as the orchestration era, the first models that understand themselves well enough to spawn sub-agents and verify work if you just prompt them to go further. Since the models are improving faster than we are, his prescription is to go bigger, and that requires getting over ourselves. His extended metaphor: developers are in their skeuomorphic phase, like pre-iOS 7 apps imitating physical objects; the terminal, git, and language-identity tribalism are familiar rather than right. Sample provocations: why can't we commit env files ("it's just how Git was built"), why do we qualify ourselves by languages, why do we guilt-merge PRs instead of deleting code. His own project tiers (side project, startup, too big) have all shifted down one level, and a new bottom tier appeared: the markdown file. His PR triage service is now literally a markdown file piped to an agent on a 9 a.m. cron that publishes a static HTML priority list by 9:20. He argued breadth is newly viable: you cannot out-feature AWS, but you can build a database platform into your product in a day or two and let users build their own missing features if the product is the right shape, citing Slack becoming the place people run agents despite itself. Closing line: if your idea doesn't feel stupid, it isn't big enough. His "delete your darlings" argument from an in-room conversation the same week is recorded in [session-recaps.md](../../notes/session-recaps.md) under the Vercel/eve entry.

## Closing Keynote: Garry Tan (Y Combinator)

[7:58:58](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=28738s). Tan, slideless, answered Theo's "what do we build" from the investor side: companies where one person does what used to take a thousand. His personal number: about 14 usable lines of code a day as a 2013 YC partner versus roughly 400x output now, and even torture-tested down it stays 8x at the floor. The leverage is not in the weights ("the 2x people and the 100x people are using the exact same Claude"); it is in how you wire the work. His mapping of harness engineering onto an org chart: a skill file is an employee, a resolver table is the org chart, filing rules are internal process, trigger evals are performance reviews; sitting down with Claude Code means hiring, training, and managing a workforce made of markdown. Evidence: winter 25 was the fastest-growing, most profitable batch in YC history with a quarter of codebases 95% AI-generated; Emergent went public launch to nine figures of ARR in eight months. He walked latent versus deterministic space (seat 800 Startup School attendees with an algorithm, let the LLM do the human part), working memory (7 plus or minus 2 for humans versus three Harry Potter books for an agent, and every institution is a prosthetic for the human limit), and the company brain: library plus librarian, where retrieval is easy and being worth retrieving from is the product. His open-source entry is GBrain (MIT-licensed, 220,000 pages written mostly by his agents), with the discipline "never do one-off work": if you have to ask twice, you failed, so skillify it. He pitched the memory layer as the green-field company to build, told the story of a father building an 80,000-file company brain for his son's rare epilepsy, and closed with "we can boil the ocean now." His AI-native company definition from an in-room conversation is in [session-recaps.md](../../notes/session-recaps.md); related folder: [vc-community](../../companies/vc-community/README.md).

## Hiring Employable Agents (Howie Liu, Airtable / Hyperagent)

[8:19:08](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=29948s). Liu (Airtable founder/CEO; Hyperagent is a product within Airtable) traced product form factors up a spectrum: completions, chat (post-InstructGPT), agents (the Anthropic definition, a model recursing on itself with open-ended tool choice, distinct from workflows), then "claws," always-on agents with heartbeat wake-ups that keep moving toward a goal for days, popularized by OpenClaw, and finally orchestration, agents coordinating fleets of other agents, which he said only works now because models hold coherence on open-ended scopes where BabyAGI and AutoGPT drifted into rabbit holes. His worked example was deliberately unglamorous: a landscaping business running nearly end to end on Hyperagent, where a triager agent scores inbound leads, a surveyor agent pulls frames from the customer's backyard video with ffmpeg in a sandbox VM, mocks up a redesign, and drafts a priced proposal that would previously only pencil out for seven-figure clients, with the human owner gating the binding quote. He agreed with Sarah Guo that value accrues where humans own policies and unblock agents, and pushed real-time learning: agents should absorb corrections from Slack or email immediately ("remember that for next time") rather than waiting for a fine-tune. The management UX he described is an orchestration control plane, a SimCity view of what every agent is working on and blocked by, with the human playing macro. Close: fleet-of-agents-native operation is table stakes for survival, plus $1,000 of inference credit for attendees at hyperagent.com (Opus 4.8, Fable 5, GLM 5.2, GPT 5.5 all offered).

## $100,000 AIE Startup Battlefield finals (presented by Hyperagent)

[8:37:21](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2cbIws9j10&t=31041s). swyx and Liu explained the setup: Hyperagent's Founding 500 program supplied the pool, 20 pre-selected startups competed earlier in the day, and three finalists pitched on the keynote stage for $100,000 in prizes ($50K/$30K/$20K), judged by Theo Browne and Joshua Xu (HeyGen) on impressive use of the technology rather than as a VC pitch. The finalists:

- **Kamad** (captions also render it "Kimode"; founders introduced as Spiro, Dom, and Nick): an agentic execution layer for physical commodity trade, a $17 trillion market still moving on paper, email, and handshakes with hundreds of billions lost to fraud. A patent-filed "CFC" state machine governs an agent swarm doing KYC and sanctions screening, document forensics, deal rooms, compliance, currency, shipping, and settlement signaling (it never touches funds; the bank moves money), plugged into Gmail, Slack, Stripe, Salesforce, and WhatsApp. They claimed a first customer, a Dubai trading company moving hundreds of millions in annual volume, said roughly 100 customers gets them to very large scale given $200M+ single-deal tickets, and answered the moat question with domain data and "the ship extinction model." Roadmap tease: becoming the bank.
- **Comment.io** (Max Windbomb and team; captions also render "common.io"): a multiplayer markdown editor built as the best document surface for people and agents together, since Google Docs has a hostile format and no real-time agent collaboration. The founders previously built collaboration features in Microsoft Word and worked at Textio. Pitch scenario: an incident room where everyone copy-pastes ideas between their own Claude sessions, versus a shared document where agents and owners exchange context live. Xu and Liu liked the broad, thin, high-token-flow shape as the inverse of Kamad's deep vertical.
- **Built by Foundry** (Weston Belgettes, ex-TikTok): teams of agents that turn content creators into founders by finding a paying pain point in their audience and building the recurring-revenue product end to end; over half a million people use products its agents built. Browne pushed back hard from experience (audience saturation, the value of variety over one owned product, "as the YouTuber who has started the second most companies... it doesn't work"), and Belgettes countered with niches like a homesteading creator whose 2 million followers pay a subscription for freeze-dryer settings, claiming zero creator churn.

**Result:** Comment.io took the $50,000 grand prize, Built by Foundry was runner-up ($30,000), and Kamad was second runner-up ($20,000). The MC then closed the conference: 4 days, 7,000 attendees, 40 tracks, with AIE New York in October and the World's Fair back next year.
