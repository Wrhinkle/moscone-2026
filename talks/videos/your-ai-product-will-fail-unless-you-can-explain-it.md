# Your AI Product Will Fail Unless You Can Explain It

> A three-part storytelling method for making complex AI products land in the time of an elevator ride.

- **Speaker:** Veronica Hylak, Hey AI
- **Video:** [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=d_Ftrl3vfV0) (AI Engineer channel; ~6m, released 2026-07-05)
- **Program:** Online Track 2026

## Summary

Hylak opens with a parody of the standard AI pitch, "an agentic AI orchestration platform for enterprise knowledge retrieval," delivered to a glazed-over buyer in an elevator. Her diagnosis: founders are shipping faster than ever and the tech is often genuinely good, but the window to make a mark lasts about one elevator ride, and pitches built from category jargon waste it. Hylak has made AI explainer videos that reached 8 million views and worked with YC startups, safety orgs, and AI teams on this problem; the talk compresses her method into three steps.

Step one is the wound, the moment the user wants to throw their laptop out a window. The rule is to never open with what you built. The first slide should immerse the buyer in their own day. Her worked example rewrites a real Series B pitch, "we built an agentic orchestration SecOps platform for enterprises," into a picture of security teams exhausted by disconnected tools, alerts in one system, tickets in another, the real investigation buried in Slack threads and screenshots. The format is about 20 seconds: identify the wound, say "we fix that," then show how.

Step two is making the product click, with a test: could a 17-year-old understand what you do? Her fastest route is attaching the product to a story people already know. An agent observability platform should not open with its category name; it should say that if McDonald's had used it, the AI drive-thru that put bacon on ice cream would never have made TikTok. She also bans abstract words nobody can picture in favor of mental images, citing "Devin, the AI software engineer" and "a smoke alarm for AI behavior" as openers that work. These are imperfect technical definitions and that is fine at this stage; the precise version comes later, to close the sale.

Step three is showing the transformation. Claims like "we improve code quality with AI" leave the buyer unable to see what changes in their world. Instead, contrast before and after: before, the support team spends 30 minutes digging through docs and tickets; after, they ask one question and get a sourced answer in 10 seconds. Hylak closes by rerunning the elevator pitch through all three steps and lands her market thesis: 15 years ago a great product might get discovered on its own, but now the clearest stories get funded, bought, and talked about, and great tech nobody understands dies quietly.
