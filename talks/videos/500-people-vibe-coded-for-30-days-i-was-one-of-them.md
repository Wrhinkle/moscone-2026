# 500 people vibe-coded for 30 days. I was one of them.

> A product designer's account of Automattic's Radical Speed Month, where two-person teams paused roadmaps for 30 days and shipped 794 projects.

- **Speaker:** Sanja Grbic, Automattic
- **Video:** [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UcYoMg-8-L8) (AI Engineer channel; ~18m, released 2026-06-26)
- **Program:** Online Track 2026

## Summary

Sanja Grbic, a product designer on Automattic's Jetpack design team for five years, reports from inside a company experiment called Radical Speed Month: pause roadmap work for 30 days, pair with a partner, and ship something real, AI welcome but not required. About a third of the 1,400-person company took part in the first round, roughly 500 people starting 794 projects. Grbic built three of them, and her talk tracks how her own role shifted project by project.

She credits the groundwork first. Automattic is fully distributed and asynchronous, so decades of work and decisions are documented, and an internal MCP server gives AI tools access to that archive for research and planning. Every employee goes through a two-week, role-specific AI enablement course, and the systems team's security setup and documentation are good enough that a non-engineer can spin up a working development environment. When the month began, Grbic had reviewed PRs and played with Claude Code but had never shipped work code.

Project one came out of a two-hour group exercise during the enablement course: a board game session manager built by two designers, an engineer, and a product lead, using only Claude Code plus Nano Banana for a 16-bit illustration of their office. Her takeaway is about the engineer who set up GitHub, taught Git basics, and divided the work: an engineer who enables others can have more impact than one who does more engineering. Project two was her main focus, a design system status tracker for Automattic's open source WordPress design system. Her proposal raised performance and maintenance doubts from the engineer on the team, so she built the full idea alone as a proof of concept: roughly two and a half weeks total, a working prototype in the first week, then a week and a half to rebuild, host, and deploy it internally and as a curated public site. The tracker pulls links from the GitHub repo, Storybook, and Figma, sorts by status, tags library names, and shows live component previews. She calls shipping it the moment she moved from designer to design engineer, enabled less by AI than by owning the whole process without negotiations and handovers.

Project three shows the compounding. In the final six days she paired with another designer and went from zero to a working iOS chat app for WooCommerce merchants: wordpress.com authentication, a Jetpack connection, a site-themed widget for shoppers, and an AI agent that scans the site, answers visitor questions in real time, and discerns when it cannot answer. Alignment was fast because both designers shaped features with users in mind, and they ran the exploration as a Claude Code project folder where every chat and idea landed in the file system, a practice she has kept.

The process claim is the durable one. Her team's habit for years was to work in Figma to high fidelity before building; now the prototype is the main artifact and Figma returns late for mood boards and visual fine-tuning. Her advice to large organizations: provide tool access, find enablers and champions, create space to experiment, and give people agency, because changing tools means shifting the human behavior behind them.
