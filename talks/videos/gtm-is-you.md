# GTM Is You
> Victoria Melnikova argues that a devtool founder's personal brand is the strongest distribution channel in 2026, drawing on interviews with PlanetScale, Sentry, and Supabase founders.

- **Speaker:** Victoria Melnikova, Evil Martians
- **Video:** [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=G6IlDzj8OjA) (AI Engineer channel; ~13m, released 2026-06-26)
- **Program:** Online Track 2026

## Summary
Melnikova runs new business at Evil Martians, a consultancy for developer tools with a blog she says reaches half a million technical readers a year and a podcast called Dev Propulsion Labs. She opens with a story about PlanetScale CEO Sam Lambert telling her at a party that a shocking number of PlanetScale customers come directly from his personal Twitter account, and that he would never delegate it. Her thesis follows from that anecdote: the most underrated competitive advantage for a startup in 2026 is not the model, the team, or the funding. It is the founder's own presence.

Her evidence comes from a framework Evil Martians calls the product market fit compass, derived from an analysis of 37 successful devtools. It plots product signal against revenue. If signal outruns revenue, the company has a distribution problem; if revenue outruns signal, a product problem. Melnikova says nine out of ten early-stage devtool clients show stronger signal than revenue, which she attributes to technical founders hating marketing. She argues the problem got harder because AI-generated outreach has flooded inboxes and feeds, so building software is no longer the bottleneck. Distribution is.

She then lays out six foundational steps: crystallize the value proposition and validate it with paying customers, understand where in a developer's life the product lives (the shelf space), pick channels from that shelf space and become the authority on the problem, map the ecosystem of friends and competitors, and distribute early, sometimes before the product is ready.

The rest of the talk is lessons from her founder interviews. First, come to San Francisco. She cites the claim that YC founders who stay in SF fundraise twice as much, twice as fast, and plays a clip of Sentry founder David Cramer saying SF access is unfair and founders should take the advantage anyway. Second, get loud once you are there. She points to the SF billboard economy, and a Typesense founder explains that as a company without VC funding milestones, hundreds of billboards became a credibility proxy; onlookers assumed the company was bigger than they had thought. Third, run events at every scale, from poker nights and boba meetups to user conferences, noting Supabase held its Supabase Select conference for a second year despite an internal no-conference culture. Fourth, do crazy marketing stunts, and treat public failure as an asset: she includes an interview with a founder who walked away from $300,000 of ARR to restart and says he should have done it sooner.

Her closing argument is that AI cannot replace a founder's personal brand, only amplify it. She quotes Zeno Rocha on staying organic rather than engineering a content strategy, and predicts that as AI improves through 2030 the pendulum swings back to artisanal craft, so founder-led sales and unique personal quirks become the durable moat.
